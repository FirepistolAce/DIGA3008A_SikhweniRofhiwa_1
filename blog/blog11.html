Justice and Ethics

When I think about what an ethical internet looks like, I think of a space that is built on respect, fairness, and care. It should be a place where people can express themselves without fear, where creators are valued, and where we take responsibility for what we post and how we treat others.

Shani Orgad (2007) writes about the internet as a moral space, based on Roger Silverstone's ideas. She talks about something called “proper distance, which means being close enough to care about others, but not so close that we lose respect for their space or identity. That stood out to me. Online, it is easy to forget there is a real person behind the screen. An ethical internet would remind us of that and make us more mindful of how we interact.

Zizi Papacharissi (2020) adds that even though the internet gives us more freedom to speak, it can also become a place where only certain voices get heard. Algorithms and platforms often push content that gets the most attention, not necessarily what matters most. For the internet to be truly ethical, it should give everyone an equal chance to be seen and heard, not just those with the most followers or reach.

Laura Stein (2008) makes a really important point, too, that even though online spaces feel public, they are usually owned by private companies. That means we do not have the same rights online as we do in real life public spaces. An ethical internet would give users more protection, more freedom of speech, and more control over their data.

As someone who is learning, creating, and sharing online, I think an ethical internet starts with us. It is not just about laws and platforms, it is about the kind of culture we build together. One where we treat each other with care, think before we post, and support a space that is safer and fairer for everyone.
